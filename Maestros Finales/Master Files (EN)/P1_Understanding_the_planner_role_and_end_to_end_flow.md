## Understanding the planner’s role within the platform

Before creating scenarios or running calculations, you need to understand where planning fits within the platform and what is expected from your role.

As a planner, you work on the part of the process that prepares future operations. Your job is not to solve real-time incidents or manage the tactical execution of today’s service. Your job is to prepare a planning baseline that other modules and other teams can use later.

Use this quick start when you are starting for the first time as a planner, when you need to clarify responsibilities between planning and operations, or when you want to understand what your work should deliver to the rest of the platform.

Before you begin, make sure that:
1. You have access to the environment with the **Planner** role or equivalent visibility.
2. You have a basic understanding that the platform is divided into several functional areas.
3. You are willing to understand the flow first, before configuring data or calculating scenarios.

To understand planning’s place within the platform:
1. Open the platform and identify the main areas related to planning, lists/rostering, and daily operations.
2. Review the general flow and understand that planning happens before day-to-day execution.
3. Think of the process in this order:
   1. **Planning** prepares the future scenario and builds a consistent foundation.
   2. **Lists/Rostering** connects that plan with real people, labor rules, and availability.
   3. **Daily operations** manages what actually happens during the current day.
4. Confirm that your role as a planner belongs to the planning layer and not to real-time operational management.
5. Use this rule of thumb as a reference: if you are preparing the future plan, you are probably working in the right place as a planner.

When you finish this section, you should understand that planning feeds downstream modules and that your work happens before tactical execution.

## Distinguishing planning from operations and vehicles

After understanding where planning sits within the platform, the next step is to understand what is yours to own—and what is not.

As a planner, you are responsible for preparing the planning logic and making sure the scenario is coherent enough for later use. This means your work includes tasks such as creating scenarios, validating the planning baseline, and preparing outputs that Scheduling, Rostering, and Operations can consume afterwards.

Before you continue, keep this idea in mind:
1. Visibility is not the same as responsibility.
2. Even if you can see a broad part of the system, you should not intervene in every domain.
3. Your role should stay focused on future planning, not dispatching or fixing live incidents.

To distinguish your role from other profiles’ work:
1. Review what you *should* do as a planner.
   1. Prepare planning scenarios.
   2. Build a coherent foundation for Scheduling.
   3. Review whether the plan logic makes sense.
   4. Deliver outputs that other modules can use later.
2. Review what does **not** belong to your role.
   1. Resolve same-day incidents in real time.
   2. Reassign drivers to cover immediate problems.
   3. Act as if you were responsible for live dispatch.
3. Compare your role with **Operations**.
   1. Planning prepares what should happen.
   2. Operations manages what is happening right now.
4. Compare your role with **Vehicles**.
   1. Planning works with vehicle-type logic and future feasibility.
   2. Vehicles works with the physical fleet, maintenance, and current availability.
5. Use this decision rule when you are unsure:
   1. If you are designing or adjusting the future plan, it belongs to planning.
   2. If you are solving a real-time operational issue, it belongs to operations.
   3. If you are updating physical fleet status or availability, it belongs to vehicles.

When you finish this section, you should be able to explain the difference between planning, operations, and vehicles without mixing responsibilities.

## Confirming what downstream teams receive from planning

Your work as a planner does not end when you create a scenario. It ends when you prepare an output that other teams and other modules can actually use.

This matters because a weak planning baseline creates downstream problems. Rostering may struggle to organize work, operations may be forced to correct more things in real time, and fleet-related decisions can become harder to interpret.

Before moving to the next quick start, make sure you understand that planning outputs should be:
1. Clear.
2. Coherent.
3. Usable.
4. Traceable.

To confirm what the rest of the platform receives from planning:
1. Review the scenario or planning output you are preparing.
2. Ask yourself whether another team could understand and use it without having to guess what you meant.
3. Confirm you are not creating something only for yourself, but a foundation that other modules will consume later.
4. Check that you are not mixing future-planning decisions with same-day operational actions.
5. Use this final rule as a reference:
   1. If you are preparing the future plan, you are in the planning domain.
   2. If you are solving a problem from today, you are already in the operations domain.
   3. If you are correcting physical fleet availability, you are in the vehicles domain.

When you finish this section, you should be ready to start building the planning baseline in the next quick start.
